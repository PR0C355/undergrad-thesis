Motivation

Large multimodal language models (MLLMs) have achieved impressive performance on standard video benchmarks, but their high scores conceal a fundamental weakness: temporal grounding, the ability to localize when events occur, not merely what they are.

This gap exists because current training data provides answer supervision but completely lacks temporal evidence supervision. Models learn which answer is correct, but never learn where in the video that answer is supported.

Gemini 3 Pro scores 88.4% on Video-MME yet achieves only 48.2% on fine-grained temporal tasks, while humans score 98.2%. (TimeBlind, 2026)

Without explicit grounding supervision, models exploit shortcuts instead of learning genuine temporal reasoning:

1
Static Shortcuts: Answering from single-frame cues that happen to correlate with correct answers, without engaging the video's temporal structure.
2
Linguistic Priors: Pattern-matching question text to answer options without watching the video at all.
3
Positional Bias: Exploiting the tendency of existing datasets to cluster evidence near video boundaries, enabling imprecise localization to score well.

Practical applications like surgical procedure review, broadcast sports analysis, security monitoring, and long-form lecture comprehension, all require models to answer and to identify precisely when supporting evidence occurs. ChronoQA addresses this gap at the training data level.

Five Training Categories

In Order of by Temporal Scope Required:
Segment-level to Full-video

1
Action Understanding
Fine-grained characterization of a video segment's content. Includes tool usage, body posture, object manipulation, and procedural steps within a single continuous action.
Scope: Single Segment
Tests: Visual Perception
2
Needle-in-Haystack
Retrieval of specific visual or textual details. Includes brand names, colors, on-screen text, counts, spatial attributes, from anywhere in the full video. Correct-answer timestamps uniformly distributed to prevent positional bias.
Scope: Any Point in the Video
Tests: Sustained Attention
3
Temporal Ordering
Reasoning about before/after relationships, event sequences, and transitions. Events may be separated by minutes, requiring a coherent temporal map of the full video.
Scope: Multi-segment
Tests: Sequence Reasoning
4
Causal Understanding
Integrates evidence from two or more non-adjacent segments to infer causation, motivation, and cross-segment patterns. No single segment contains sufficient information to answer correctly.
Scope: Multiple Disjoint Segments
TTests: Cross-segment Synthesis
5
General Understanding
Holistic comprehension of the video's overarching narrative, primary subject, core message, or tone. Correct answers are only derivable after processing the video in its entirety.
Scope: Full Video
Tests: Global Narrative Reasoning
Automated Generation Pipeline

ChronoQA is produced by a fully automated pipeline, requiring no manual annotation, enabling scaling to hundreds of thousands of training examples.

1. YouTube Crawl
Filter by duration (5–60 min), content type (instructional, narrative, procedural, sports, vlogs), and Creative Commons licensing.
↓
2. Visual Diversity Filter
Compute per-video delta score from CLIP embeddings & cosine similarity across sampled frames. Discard low-motion, static-content videos.
↓
3. Segmentation
Split each video into fixed 10s clips. Experiments with 5s, 10s, 20s, and 30s segments show 10s strikes the optimal balance of temporal resolution vs. computational cost.
↓
4. Caption Generation
Qwen3-VL-30B via vLLM at $1 FPS with token budget. Each segment yields a timestamped free-form caption. Token budget determines representation space for visual detail per frame-pair.
↓
5. QA Generation
Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Thinking with Pydantic-enforced structured JSON. Five category-specific prompt templates, with 3 questions per category per video. ~98% generation success rate.
↓
6. Validation & Cleaning
Pattern matching removes time-referencing questions. Adjacent timestamps <2s apart are merged. Timestamp jitter of random(1,3)s applied to start and end to increase annotation variance.
↓
7. Distractor Generation
Separate LLM pass generates 4 plausible distractors per question, grounded in ±30s local caption context. Distractors match the correct answer in length, casing, and semantic category.
↓
8. Blind Filtering
Multiple text-only LLMs (including Qwen3-Next-80B) are presented with question + options only. Questions answerable by multiple models are discarded. This ensures visual necessity of every retained example.
Caption Token Budget

Visual token count per video segment is controlled via the $T_{\text{video}}$ parameter, which caps total pixel input across all sampled frames:

Pixel cap and per frame-pair budget $$P_{\text{video}} = T_{\text{video}} \times 32^2 = T_{\text{video}} \times 1{,}024$$ $$T_{\text{fp}} = T_{\text{video}} / t \quad \text{(tokens per frame-pair)}$$

At $1 FPS$ with temporal compression factor of $2$, a $t$-second segment produces $\frac{t}{2}$ frame-pairs. Higher $T_{\text{video}}$ yields finer detail but increases KV-cache pressure and reduces inference concurrency via PagedAttention. Token budgets of 32, 64, and 128 tokens per frame-pair were evaluated. 64 was selected as the production setting.

Training Objective

Each ChronoQA example supports a dual training objective, jointly optimizing answer correctness and temporal localization quality:

Answer Supervision: Cross-entropy $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ans}} = \text{CrossEntropy}(\hat{a},\, a^*)$$ $$\text{where } a^* \in \{A, B, C, D, E\}$$
Temporal Grounding: IoU-based loss $$\text{tIoU} = \frac{|P \cap G|}{|P \cup G|} \times 100\%$$ $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{grd}} = 1 - \text{tIoU}(\text{predicted},\, [t_s,\, t_e])$$
Joint objective $$\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ans}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{grd}}$$
A model trained on answer labels alone can reach high accuracy by exploiting shortcuts. The grounding loss forces the model to internalize where the answer is supported, producing representations that generalize to genuinely novel temporal queries.

Grounded Accuracy (G-Acc) measures joint success: A correct answer and $\text{tIoU} \geq 0.5$ on the predicted interval, providing a unified metric for downstream evaluation of trained models.

Quality Control
✓
Blind Filtering: Performance of multiple text-only LLMs cannot exceed random chance. Visual engagement is structurally required, not assumed.
✓
Timestamp Jitter: ±1–3s random expansion of ground-truth intervals prevents models from memorizing exact 10s clip boundaries.
✓
Uniform Timestamp Distribution: Needle-in-Haystack correct-answer timestamps are placed evenly across the full video timeline, eliminating start/end positional bias found in previous works.
✓
Balanced Answer Positions: Correct answers are uniformly distributed across $A-E$, preventing learned position shortcuts.
✓
Visual Diversity Filtering:A CLIP-based delta score removes static, low-motion videos to ensure that every example demands temporal attention.
✓
Semantically Grounded Distractors: Wrong options are real events that have occurred in the same video, forcing contrastive temporal discrimination rather than pattern matching.
Downstream Use Cases
Supervised Fine-Tuning
Train VLMs on answer + timestamp pairs to instill temporal grounding as an explicit learned capability on top of pretrained foundations.
RLHF / Preference Data
Use grounded vs. ungrounded correct answers as preference pairs for reward modeling. Grounded answers preferred over correct, but unlocalized, ones.
Pretraining Augmentation
Mix into large-scale video pretraining corpora to improve temporal awareness from the ground up, prior to task-specific fine-tuning.
Probing & Diagnostics
Held-out split supports controlled evaluation of temporal grounding across all five reasoning categories and video duration ranges.
Curriculum Learning
Five categories naturally ordered by difficulty, training on Action Understanding first, and progressing towards Causal and General as temporal reasoning matures.
Domain Adaptation
Instructional and procedural video subsets directly support domain-specific fine-tuning for medical, legal, and educational video applications.

Distractor Design Philosophy

Each question receives exactly 4 wrong answers generated by a dedicated LLM pass using a specialist prompt. Distractors are designed to be plausible to an inattentive viewer while remaining clearly incorrect:

→
Same-category Constraint: Distractors belong to the same semantic category as the correct answer. If the answer is a color, all distractors are colors. If an action, all distractors describe actions. This prevents trivial elimination by type.
→
Scene-grounded Alternatives: Distractors are drawn from the same scene. Similar objects, nearby people, or adjacent details that are present but not the correct answer. They must feel like they could have been the answer given the same scene.
→
Anti-shortcut Formatting: All distractors match the casing style and approximate length of the correct answer. Length asymmetry is a well-known test-taking cue that allows correct guessing without visual understanding.

A model with only text sees five same-category, same-length, scene-plausible options. Without visual grounding there is no basis for discrimination beyond baseline random chance (~20%).

Contributions
1
ChronoQA Training Corpus: The first large-scale dataset pairing long-form video QA with verified timestamp supervision across five temporal reasoning categories, sourced from 40,000+ YouTube videos of 5–60 minutes duration.
2
Scalable Automated Pipeline: Fully automated generation from crawl to quality-filtered training examples via Qwen3-VL captioning, structured QA generation, and multi-model blind filtering. No manual annotation required.
3
Dual Training Objective: Answer & grounding supervision per instance directly targets the gap between QA accuracy and temporal localization ability, enabling models trained on ChronoQA to be evaluated with the joint G-Acc metric.
